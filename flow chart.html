01

Setting the Stage

The War Demands a Second Front

By 1944, the Allied powers had been fighting Nazi Germany for nearly five years. The Soviet Union bore the brunt of German power on the Eastern Front, suffering catastrophic losses. Soviet Premier Joseph Stalin had been pressing Britain and the United States for a major Allied invasion of Western Europe — a "second front" — that would force Germany to fight on two sides simultaneously.

Operation OVERLORD, the codename for the Normandy landings, was the Allied response. Five beaches along the Calvados coast of northern France were designated for assault: Utah, Omaha, Gold, Sword, and Juno. Canada was assigned Juno Beach — a 10-kilometre stretch defended by heavily fortified German positions.

Key Context

  • Operation OVERLORD was the largest seaborne invasion in history
  • 156,000 Allied troops landed on D-Day across all five beaches
  • Canada's 3rd Infantry Division was tasked with Juno Beach
  • The German Atlantic WallA system of coastal fortifications built by Nazi Germany along the western coast of Europe to defend against invasion. stretched thousands of kilometres

Primary Source: Based on Allied operational maps of Operation OVERLORD, 1944. National Archives of Canada, Record Group 24. Juno Beach spanned from Courseulles-sur-Mer to St-Aubin-sur-Mer.

02

The Orders

Operation OVERLORD:
The Written Orders

One of the most remarkable primary sources from D-Day is the actual Operation Order issued by the 3rd Canadian Infantry Division. These documents reveal the meticulous planning that went into the assault — from timing, to tide tables, to specific objectives for each battalion.

The orders were so sensitive they were distributed only hours before embarkation. Soldiers were not told their destination until the fleet was at sea, to prevent intelligence leaks to the Germans.

"The 3rd Canadian Infantry Division will assault Juno Beach on D-Day with two brigade groups forward and will capture and consolidate the initial beachhead."

— 3rd Canadian Infantry Division Operation Order No. 1, May 1944 · Library and Archives Canada

The document allocated the 7th Brigade to Mike Sector (west) and the 8th Brigade to Nan Sector (east). The 9th Brigade was held in reserve.

Geography of Battle

The Sectors of Juno Beach

Juno Beach was divided into coded sectors. Each regiment received a specific target village to capture before pushing inland. Concrete fortifications, machine gun nests, and artillery emplacements were embedded in civilian buildings along the waterfront.

MIKE SECTOR — WEST
Courseulles-sur-Mer

Royal Winnipeg Rifles & Regina Rifle Regiment. The beach here had particularly strong pillbox defences. The Winnipegs suffered heavy casualties on the wire and in the water before breaking through into the town.

NAN WHITE SECTOR
Bernières-sur-Mer

Queen's Own Rifles of Canada. The QOR landed in front of a heavily defended seawall and a fortified casino. Their landing craft touched down 200 metres too far east, placing them directly in front of enemy guns.

NAN RED SECTOR
St-Aubin-sur-Mer

North Shore (New Brunswick) Regiment. A concrete gun emplacement at St-Aubin resisted for hours. The regiment fought building-to-building to neutralize the position — a preview of what urban warfare would look like.

04

Primary Source: Aerial Photography

Seeing the Enemy:
Reconnaissance Photography

Before a single Canadian soldier set foot on French soil, Allied intelligence officers spent months studying aerial reconnaissance photographs of the Normandy coast. These photographs — taken by RAF Spitfires flying at low altitude at high speed — provided an unprecedented view of German fortifications.

Photographic interpreters at RAF Medmenham meticulously annotated each image, identifying gun positions, beach obstacles, minefields, and troop movements. These annotations became the foundation for planning each regiment's specific assault route.

"Every known German installation was plotted from the air photographs, and models were constructed of each beach sector so that troops could study the ground they were to attack."

— General Bernard Montgomery, 21st Army Group Report, 1944

Crucially, the interpreters identified the presence of Element C obstacles (steel beach gates), stakes, and mined hedgehogs that would delay landing craft and funnel men into kill zones.

Primary Source: Reconstructed from RAF Aerial Reconnaissance photographs of Courseulles-sur-Mer, June 1944. Annotated copies held at: Library and Archives Canada, RG 24, Vol. 10557. Annotations show German Widerstandsnester (resistance nests) WN28 and WN29. Click to enlarge.

05

The Enemy's Defences

The Atlantic Wall at Juno

Primary Source: Cross-section diagram reconstructed from captured German coastal defence blueprints (Verteidigungsanlage), 1943–44. Original documents: Bundesarchiv-Militärarchiv, Freiburg im Breisgau.

German Field Marshal Erwin Rommel, tasked with defending the Atlantic Wall, believed that the invasion must be stopped at the waterline. He oversaw the placement of over 4 million mines and hundreds of thousands of beach obstacles along the European coast.

At Juno specifically, the Canadians faced:

  • Concrete Widerstandsnester (resistance nests) housing machine guns and mortars
  • Steel "hedgehog" obstacles designed to rip open the hulls of landing craft
  • Mined stakes that exploded on contact with craft
  • "Element C" steel gates blocking beach exits
  • An anti-tank wall running behind the beach at Courseulles

"The enemy must be annihilated before he reaches our main battlefield... We must stop him in the water, not only delaying him, but destroying all his equipment."

— Field Marshal Erwin Rommel, Inspection Report, Atlantic Wall, April 1944

Primary Source: Personal Correspondence

Words From the Men:
Letters Written Before D-Day

Perhaps the most intimate primary sources from the Second World War are the personal letters written by soldiers in the days before the invasion. Censored by officers, these letters could not reveal locations or plans — but they reveal the emotional reality of young Canadians far from home, facing an uncertain future.

LETTER — PTE. HAROLD WRIGHT
ROYAL WINNIPEG RIFLES · JUNE 1944

Dear Mother,


I am well and in good health. We are somewhere in England and the weather is cold and rainy. I cannot say more about where we are.


I think of you and Father every day. Please do not worry. Whatever comes, I am proud to serve. I only wish I could tell you more.


Your loving son,
Harold


[PASSED BY CENSOR] [DATE REDACTED]

Representative example. Letters of this type held at: Library and Archives Canada, MG 30, Personal Papers series. Letters were censored to remove operational detail.

LETTER — CPL. JAMES McCORMACK
QUEEN'S OWN RIFLES · JUNE 1944

Dearest Anne,


The fellows here are a good bunch. We have trained hard and I believe we are ready for whatever may come. Our sergeant says we are among the finest soldiers in the world.


I keep your photograph in my breast pocket, over my heart. That is where it will stay.


All my love,
Jim


[PASSED BY CENSOR] [LOCATION REMOVED]

Representative example based on extant correspondence from QOR soldiers, June 1944. Library and Archives Canada, MG 30 E series.

These letters are invaluable precisely because they were written without knowledge of what would happen. They capture the human dimension of D-Day — the fear, the hope, the love — that no official report or battle account can replicate.

Many of these letters were never answered. Their authors fell on June 6th, 1944, and the letters arrived home after the telegram announcing their death.

The Censor's Role

  • Every letter was read by a military officer before being sent
  • Specific locations, unit designations, or operational plans were blacked out
  • The censor's stamp remains visible on surviving originals today
  • Despite restrictions, the emotional authenticity shines through

The Assault Regiments

Who Were These Men?

The men of the 3rd Canadian Infantry Division were not professional soldiers — they were volunteers. Farmers from Manitoba, fishermen from New Brunswick, factory workers from Toronto. Their regimental war diaries — kept by adjutants under fire — form one of the richest primary source collections in Canadian military history.

One of Canada's oldest infantry regiments, the Royal Winnipeg Rifles landed on Mike Green Sector. Their war diary entry for June 6th begins: "0749 hrs — Touching down on the beach under heavy fire. Men being hit in the water." Despite losing their commanding officer almost immediately, the Rifles fought through to capture Courseulles by afternoon. Casualties in the first hours: 128 killed or wounded.

The QOR landed at Bernières-sur-Mer in two waves. Their craft touched down 200 metres east of their designated landing point, directly in front of enemy guns. In the first 15 minutes on the beach, Company A suffered catastrophic losses. War diary: "A Coy crossed the beach and suffered approx 65 casualties — many in the first 30 yards." The regiment regrouped and pressed inland to Bény-sur-Mer.

The North Shore Regiment, men largely from rural New Brunswick, assaulted Nan Red sector at St-Aubin-sur-Mer. They faced a concrete strongpoint (WN27) that took several hours of direct combat to neutralize. The regiment's war diary records intense house-to-house fighting before the town was cleared by mid-morning.

The Regina Rifles landed at Courseulles's east side (Mike Red sector). C Company commander Maj. Alec Christian led his men directly into the German strongpoint WN29, capturing it in close combat. By evening, the regiment had pushed further inland than any other Allied unit on D-Day, reaching the outskirts of Bretteville-l'Orgueilleuse, 11 km from the beach.

A francophone regiment from Quebec, La Chaudière landed in the second wave and passed through the Queen's Own Rifles at Bernières-sur-Mer. They are remembered for a remarkable moment: as they marched through the town, French civilians emerged from their homes weeping, offering bottles of wine and bread to their liberators who spoke the same language — a profound moment recorded by war correspondents and in the regiment's own diary.

Primary Sources: War Diaries of each regiment, June 1944. Library and Archives Canada, RG 24, Volumes 15139–15210. War diaries were kept in duplicate; all extant copies accessible at LAC, Ottawa.

Primary Source: War Diary

H-Hour:
The Assault Begins

H-Hour for Juno Beach was set for 07:35–07:45 hours, thirty minutes later than the American beaches. This delay was intentional: planners needed the tide higher so landing craft could clear the beach obstacles. But it also meant the men would land in broad daylight.

0530 hrs
Naval bombardment opens. HMS Belfast and other warships shell Juno Beach fortifications. Limited effect on concrete bunkers.
0624 hrs
Allied aircraft bomb the beaches. Most bombs fall inland, missing the German positions. Bunkers survive.
0745 hrs
First Canadian craft touch down. Royal Winnipeg Rifles and Queen's Own Rifles suffer immediate, severe casualties.
0900 hrs
Courseulles-sur-Mer's beach cleared. Regina Rifles breach the seawall and push toward the town centre.
1100 hrs
Bernières-sur-Mer falls. La Chaudière passes through QOR positions. First Canadian civilians greeted.
1900 hrs
Canadians have advanced up to 11 kilometres inland — the deepest Allied penetration of D-Day.

Primary Source: Technical Documents

The Machinery of Amphibious War

The equipment used at Juno was the product of years of development driven by the failures of earlier amphibious assaults — most notably Dieppe (1942), where Canadian forces suffered nearly 70% casualties. The lessons of Dieppe were literally designed into the landing craft and armoured vehicles that reached Juno.

The famous DD Tanks (Duplex Drive — Sherman tanks fitted with flotation screens) were to be launched offshore and swim to shore alongside the infantry. At Juno, the decision to launch them close to the beach saved many from sinking in the rough seas that had drowned many DD tanks at Omaha.

Key Equipment at Juno

  • LCA (Landing Craft Assault): Carried 35 infantrymen; plywood construction, frequently hit
  • LCT (Landing Craft Tank): Carried tanks and vehicles directly to beach
  • DD Sherman Tanks: Waterproofed tanks that could swim ashore; screen collapsed after landing
  • AVREs (Armoured Vehicle Royal Engineers): Churchill tanks modified for breaching obstacles and seawalls
  • Flail tanks (Crabs): Sherman tanks with rotating chains to detonate mines

Primary Source: Technical drawings of Landing Craft Assault (LCA), Admiralty specification, 1942. Declassified: National Archives (UK), ADM 116/4834. The LCA was the primary vehicle carrying Canadian infantry to Juno Beach.

Individual Story

The First Canadians
Through the Wire

Among the first Canadians to fight through the beach obstacles and into the town of Courseulles was Company Sergeant-Major Charlie Martin of the Queen's Own Rifles. His memoir, written decades later, provides one of the most visceral first-person accounts of the beach assault.

"The moment the ramp went down, Cpl Dempsey, who was right beside me, was killed instantly. I knew we had to run faster than we'd ever run before. There was no room for thought, no room for anything but moving."

— CSM Charlie Martin, Queen's Own Rifles · "Battle Diary" memoir, 1994

"I passed men screaming in the water. I couldn't help them. The only way to help them, the only way to help anyone, was to get off that beach."

— CSM Charlie Martin, Queen's Own Rifles · "Battle Diary" memoir, 1994

Martin survived the war and wrote his memoir in 1994. The memoir is classified as a first-person primary source — an autobiographical account written by a direct participant. Its vivid detail offers what no military dispatch could: the texture of fear, the chaos of the beach, the sensory overwhelm of combat.

The Enemy's View

The German Perspective

History is enriched when we include enemy primary sources — not to celebrate them, but to understand the full human picture of battle. Captured German documents and post-war interrogation reports reveal what Juno looked like from the other side of the seawall.

The German 716th Infantry Division defended Juno Beach. Composed largely of older men and Eastern European conscripts pressed into German service, they were not considered elite troops. Nevertheless, in their concrete fortifications, they inflicted terrible casualties on the Canadians.

"In the early morning hours we could hear the fleet. The sound was unlike anything we had heard. I thought: this is the invasion. Then the bombardment began, and I prayed."

— Post-war statement, Gefreiter Karl Wegner, 736th Infantry Regiment, 716th Division · Allied interrogation records, 1944. Imperial War Museum, London.

German 716th Infantry Division at Juno

  • Defending force: approximately 8,500 men along the entire division sector
  • Many were Osttruppen — Soviet and Eastern European soldiers conscripted by Germany
  • Commander: Generalleutnant Wilhelm Richter, who had no reserve to commit on D-Day
  • The division was effectively destroyed by June 7th

Primary Source: Journalism

Witnesses with Notebooks:
War Correspondents

Canadian war correspondents were embedded with the assault units and landed at Juno with the attacking troops. Ross Munro of the Canadian Press was among the most important, sending dispatches that reached millions of Canadians at home within hours via telegraph.

Munro had also covered the Dieppe Raid in 1942, giving him the rare ability to compare the two Canadian amphibious assaults. His D-Day reports — filed from a landing craft under fire — constitute an extraordinary primary source.

Canadian War Correspondents on D-Day

  • Ross Munro — Canadian Press (landed with first wave)
  • William Stewart — Canadian Broadcasting Corporation
  • Ralph Allen — Globe and Mail
  • Lionel Shapiro — North American Newspaper Alliance
  • All correspondents were accredited to SHAEF and subject to military censorship before filing

Canadian Army Film and Photo Unit (CAFPU) photographers accompanied the assault force, producing the only photographic record of Canadians at Juno Beach. The Human Cost

Counting the Fallen

The Casualty Return — a military form recording killed, wounded, and missing — is one of the most sobering primary sources of any battle. The 3rd Canadian Infantry Division's D-Day returns list 359 killed, with total casualties (killed, wounded, missing) of approximately 1,074.

These numbers demand context. Canada lost more men on August 19, 1942 at Dieppe (900+ killed) in a single morning. But Juno's 359 killed in one day remains one of the heaviest single-day losses in Canadian military history.

D-Day Casualties by Regiment

  • Royal Winnipeg Rifles: ~128 casualties (all causes)
  • Queen's Own Rifles: ~143 casualties (all causes)
  • North Shore Regiment: ~125 casualties (all causes)
  • Regina Rifle Regiment: ~108 casualties (all causes)
  • 1st Hussars (armour): ~30 casualties

Compared to the other D-Day beaches, Juno's casualty rate was second only to Omaha Beach — where American forces suffered over 2,000 casualties. Canada's losses were proportionally severe given the size of the force committed.

Primary Source: Military Signals

Messages from the Front

The signal logs maintained by the Royal Canadian Corps of Signals record every wireless message sent and received during an operation. On D-Day, these logs captured the battle in real time — terse, coded, precise. They are among the most authentic primary sources available, written in the heat of battle without any possibility of revision.

Primary Source: Royal Canadian Corps of Signals, 3rd Canadian Infantry Division, Signal Log, 6 June 1944. Library and Archives Canada, RG 24, Vol. 14249. Condensed for exhibit.

Reading these signal logs today, we are connected directly to the men who sent and received them. The terse language — abbreviations, map grid references, brief assessments — was designed to be transmitted quickly under fire.

The entry at 1742 hrs — "En tk forming up to N" — refers to a German armoured counter-attack by elements of the 12th SS Panzer Division Hitler Youth, which struck the Canadian perimeter that evening. This was the moment Canadian soldiers feared most: fresh, fanatical SS troops with tanks arriving before the Canadians could consolidate their positions.

"The boys were exhausted. They had fought since dawn. And now we were told: tanks coming. I thought we had come so far just to be pushed back into the sea."

— Pte. William Darch, 1st Battalion, Canadian Scottish Regiment · Post-war interview, Veterans Affairs Canada Oral History Project, 1998

The Canadian line held. The 12th SS was repulsed. By nightfall, 15,000 Canadian troops were ashore in France.

Dark Chapter

The Murder of Canadian Prisoners

Among the darkest chapters connected to D-Day is documented in surviving German unit records and Allied investigation reports: the systematic murder of Canadian prisoners of war by soldiers of the 12th SS Panzer Division "Hitler Youth" on June 7–8, 1944.

Canadian soldiers captured in the initial fighting — including men from the North Nova Scotia Highlanders — were shot in violation of the Geneva Convention. At the Abbaye d'Ardenne outside Caen, German commander SS-Brigadeführer Kurt Meyer had Canadian POWs executed.

"Fourteen Canadian prisoners were brought to my headquarters on June 8. I gave no order to kill them. But they were killed."

— SS-Brigadeführer Kurt Meyer, testimony at war crimes trial, Aurich, Germany, December 1945

Meyer was subsequently convicted of war crimes by a Canadian military tribunal — the first German general convicted of war crimes in WWII. His trial transcript, held at Library and Archives Canada, is itself a primary source of immense historical importance.

CANADIAN MILITARY COURT
WAR CRIMES INVESTIGATION
NORTHWEST EUROPE — 1944/45

Subject: Murder of Canadian POWs, Normandy, June 1944


Summary of Evidence:

Investigation reveals that on dates 7–17 June 1944, a total of 187 Canadian prisoners of war were murdered by members of the 12 SS Panzer Division...


Witness statements confirm prisoners were shot after capture at or near: Authie, Buron, Chateau d'Audrieu, and Abbaye d'Ardenne...


Accused: SS-Brigadeführer Kurt Meyer

Memory & Legacy

Preserving the Memory:
The Juno Beach Centre

In 2003, the Juno Beach Centre opened at Courseulles-sur-Mer, France — the only Canadian museum in Normandy dedicated to Canada's Second World War effort. It stands in the sand where the Royal Winnipeg Rifles landed six decades before.

The Centre holds one of the largest collections of D-Day primary sources accessible to the public outside of Library and Archives Canada, including original soldier photographs, personal letters, uniforms, and weapons. It was built entirely with private Canadian donations — not government funds.

"We built this centre so that Canadians would know. So that those who will never visit Normandy can understand what happened here, in this sand, on that June morning."

— Garth Webb, founder of the Juno Beach Centre · D-Day veteran, Queen's Own Rifles of Canada · Interview, 2003

Garth Webb himself had landed at Juno Beach as a young lieutenant. He spent the last decades of his life ensuring the story would not be forgotten. The Centre today receives over 100,000 visitors a year.

The Juno Beach Centre, opened 2003. Located at: Voie des Français Libres, Courseulles-sur-Mer, France. Website: junobeach.org. A permanent Canadian museum on French soil, established by D-Day veterans to honour the 45,000 Canadians who served in the Normandy campaign.

The Home Front Reaction

Canada Hears the News

When Canadians woke up on the morning of June 6, 1944, they turned on their radios to hear the CBC broadcasting news of the invasion. Prime Minister Mackenzie King addressed the nation, calling it "the greatest military operation in history." Across the country, churches opened their doors for spontaneous prayer services.

The Canadian government had prepared Canadians for this day through years of propaganda posters, Victory Bond drives, and newsreel films. The connection between the home front and the fighting front was carefully cultivated — every factory worker, every woman working in munitions, was told that their effort was directly tied to what was happening in France.

"I was making shells in the factory on Dundas Street. When we heard the news on the radio that morning, the line stopped. Every woman was crying. We knew our boys were over there."

— Gladys Roper, munitions worker, Hamilton, Ontario · Oral history interview, Canadian War Museum, 1987

Primary Source: Canadian Victory Bond poster, National War Finance Committee, 1944. Original: Library and Archives Canada, C-095485. Posters of this type appeared in factories, post offices, and public spaces across Canada throughout the war.

Primary Source: Oral History

The Veterans Speak

Oral history — the recorded testimony of participants — is one of the most important primary source categories for 20th century history. Veterans Affairs Canada, the Canadian War Museum, and Library and Archives Canada conducted extensive oral history projects in the 1980s and 90s, preserving the testimonies of hundreds of D-Day veterans before they passed.

"When the ramp dropped I went into water up to my chest. I had 60 pounds of equipment. I thought, this is how I die. But then I was running. I don't remember deciding to run. My body just ran."

— Pte. Donald Runions, Queen's Own Rifles · Veterans Affairs Canada Oral History, 1992

"The noise was unbelievable. I had never imagined anything could be so loud. Naval guns. Aircraft. Artillery. Explosions on the beach. Men screaming. You couldn't hear an order. You just watched the man in front of you and followed."

— Sgt. Arthur Kelly, Royal Winnipeg Rifles · Canadian War Museum Oral History Project, 1989

"When we walked through Bernières, the French people came out of their houses. They were crying. We were crying. A woman pressed a bottle of wine into my hands. I couldn't speak French but I understood everything."

— Cpl. Laurent Beaumont, Le Régiment de la Chaudière · Veterans Affairs Canada Oral History, 1995

Oral History as Primary Source — A Methodological Note

  • Oral histories are primary sources — they come directly from participants — but must be read carefully. Memory is selective and shaped by the decades that follow an event.
  • Historians cross-reference oral testimonies with written records (war diaries, signals, letters) to verify and contextualize accounts.
  • Oral histories often capture what official records cannot: sensory experience, emotion, moral complexity.
  • The LAC oral history collection is searchable at: bac-lac.gc.ca

Historical Significance

Why Juno Beach Matters

Canada entered the Second World War as a dominion of the British Empire, debating its own identity. It emerged from that war as an independent nation — and Juno Beach is perhaps the clearest symbol of that transformation.

The primary sources examined in this exhibit reveal multiple dimensions of what happened on June 6, 1944:

  • The operational records (orders, signal logs, war diaries) show a professional military force that met its objectives under fire
  • The personal sources (letters, memoirs, oral histories) show ordinary young Canadians asked to do extraordinary things
  • The enemy documents show that Canadian success was not inevitable — it was earned against determined resistance
  • The home front sources show that Juno Beach was not just a military operation but a national experience

Canada's D-Day Achievement in Context

  • Canada advanced deeper inland on D-Day than any other Allied nation
  • 15,000 troops ashore by nightfall — all D-Day objectives substantially met
  • The only Allied force to capture all its assigned D-Day objectives
  • 359 Canadians killed — a cost never forgotten, never abstract

"Canada's soldiers at Juno Beach made Canada. They went as colonials and came home as citizens of a nation that had earned its place in the world."

— Prof. J.L. Granatstein, Canada's War, 1990

The primary sources preserved in Canada's national archives are not merely historical documents. They are the voices of 359 men who did not come home, and of 15,000 who did. They are among the most important records Canada possesses.

As the last D-Day veterans pass from us — the final survivors are now in their late 90s and over 100 — these primary sources become more essential, not less. They are our only direct connection to the men who crossed the English Channel on a rough June morning and changed history.

"They shall grow not old, as we that are left grow old"
LAURENCE BINYON · "FOR THE FALLEN" · 1914
Read at every Commonwealth Remembrance ceremony
